The Municipal Authority of Westmoreland County has a poorly designed website. Violating Krug’s first law of usability, the website makes me think. Firstly, the website is overcomplicated and has a high cognitive load. At first glance, I was paralyzed—before the jumbotron animated, I could not figure out where to look. Secondly, I should be able to automatically find the sites navigation options to accomplish a given task. However, due to the lack of white space, lack of simplicity, and lack of contrast, the content is hard to find. Finally, the website overuses animation thus overriding any potential hierarchical structures which could allow users to easily find the elements necessary to complete a given task. For example, while attempting to find the navigation bar, my eye was drawn to the animated jumbotron after being initially overwhelmed by the colors, then to the red emergency button before being drawn away by animated stream of articles. Overall, the websites visible language is jumbled and hard to decipher.

A Positive Perspective on New Streaming Services

You can watch your favorite shows today, but will you be able tomorrow? In a world where cable television is slowly dying and streaming services have come to the forefront, power moves are being made by big players, such as Disney, to establish their own territory in the streaming industry (Cruz). These power moves will likely disrupt the delicate balance which keeps us in streaming stasis; however, I believe the results of the disruption could ultimately be beneficial.

In the short term, users will likely get the short end of the stick. Following Disney’s lead, many companies will try their hand at independence, remove their content from current services, and launch their own streaming services; however, their content will likely remain relatively similar to their current offerings resulting in only additional costs for consumers (Cruz). Yet, as long as consumers refuse to buy into their cash grabs, we will likely see a scramble amongst an array of new and old streaming services toward innovation through the production of original media, and this is a win for consumers.

On the one hand, if the companies succeed in creating new original content and drawing in customers, the content would have to be that of quality. In which case, the average consumer would be happy to enjoy their new favorites in exchange for a bunch of content they generally did not watch anyway. On the other hand, if the majority of companies fail at innovation (which is most apt to happen) and become requisitioned, their reabsorption will create an oligopoly. That word is scary, but as it is now we are more or less in an oligopoly with Netflix, Hulu, and Amazon ruling the steaming scene. This newly refined oligopoly; however, will likely be more established and competitive. In time, I believe the oligopoly would adopt a model similar to that of the big three fast food chains--Wendy’s, Burger King, and McDonalds. They would each offer similar services, at similar prices, but with different brands and identities (Lufkin). To compete they would likely result to a similar model of aiming to acquire large user bases with low prices and maintaining them by utilizing periodic content creation to create brand loyalty.

In either scenario, customers have to be gained through unique and original content which is often hard to come by. Therefore, by breaking the current stasis, we will inevitably round around to at worst, services akin to what we already have or at best one that is cheaper, more content rich, and more competitive. According to Snider, “Netflix has already signed several creative deals”, and Amazon likewise has ”landed The Walking Dead creator Robert Kirkman and Skybound Entertainment...to develop exclusive TV series”, so despite my generally pessimistic views, I’ve decided to stay positive and anticipate the plethora of new quality entertainment which will be created in the upcoming steam wars.

Writing Assignment 1

Unfortunately, in the world of business, it is very easy to allow oneself to be taken advantage of by clients. An excuse here, a dodged call there, and, before you know it, clients are months overdue on payments for your completed work. This is a reality for many startups, small business owners, and freelancers who often struggle to keep their lives and/or businesses afloat due to delinquency. Bianca Board and her business Web123 were one such a business as this until Bianca introduced a patient yet strict payment system. Board’s patient yet strict payment system is a very good model for any aspiring business.

Board’s system is centered around the establishment of clear boundaries and confidence. Clear boundaries, which include lengths of leniency, defined penalties, and methods of payment, help both parties by clearing any ambiguity of expectation. With clear boundaries, empathetic hearts can prevent their propensity toward over-leniency, and clients can more easily fulfill their end of the bargain due to the penalties of procrastination. When paired with a firm demeanor, patience, and confidence, Board’s model is not only effective and professional but admirable and endearing. It can enable businesses to say no, make more efficient deals, and get that which they are due.
